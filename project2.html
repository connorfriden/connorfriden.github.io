Swap and Shop

A virtual car part swap meet

Joey Spreha - Team Leader

Connor Friden - Researcher/Designer

Wendy Adams - Researcher/Designer

About

At the beginning of our semester, students were tasked to pitch an app idea to our Interaction Design class, and form groups to create one. My team leader proposed the idea of Swap and Shop, a virtual car part swap meet, due to his interest in cars. I chose to be a part of Joey's group because I am also interested in cars and car parts. As a team member, my job was to assist my team leader with any of the tasks at hand. I was also tasked with assisting my fellow group member, Wendy with anything our team leader needed us to get done. Our team leader gave us a lot of freedom in designing our app, so I used my artistic abilities to help my group to develop an aesthetically pleasing product. Aside from that, I was also tasked with taking notes during both rounds of our user testing.

Goal Directed Design

For this project, our team followed the ideas of Goal Directed Design, developed by Alan Cooper. These ideas lay out the framework for the project, and include the planning, research, modeling, testing and the refining of our work. Goal Directed Design must be followed in a specific order to be accomplished correctly.

Literature Review and Competitive Audit

Once groups were formed, and our team had established what the project was we did a Literature Review. Essentially, this is looking at and other pieces written on our subject, in this case, buying car parts. The purpose of this is to see what prior research has been done, and what we are looking at in terms of creating an app.

The Competitive Audit is used to take a look at our competition. We used this to see what they did similarily, differently, and what we could do to set ourselves apart from them.

First Round of Interviews

After the initial research, we set out to ask people what they would want in a Car part app, and what current methods they use to buy car parts. Since not everyone purchases car parts, we decided the best way to go about this interviewing people at car shows. We went to Caffeine and Octane in Atlanta, and got lots of information from true car people there.

From our interviews and prior research, we found that there is no one way everyone buys parts, and that many sites exist. However, many people have to go to specific sites for some parts, or don't trust others as much. This is where we wanted to capitalize, by being a trustworthy location to buy and sell, and have everything all in one place.

PRODUCT BACKLOG/HYPOTHESIS TABLE (SPRINT 1)

As a team, we created a product backlog and multiple hypothesis statements that we knew would change as we progressed through the project. Based on our hypothesis statements, we came up with business outcomes that would get our product out into the market and make it a viable business. Our product backlog underwent many changes throughout Sprint 1 and was finalized during Sprint 2. Once we conducted interviews and did more research, we were able to complete the backlog and remove anything that didnâ€™t belong.

Personas

We developed two primary personas, a buyer and a seller, that we would create the app for. Combined with their expectations, we created a context scenario for when someone would use the app. These allowed us to make sure we were designing for the right people at all times.

Low Fidelity Prototyping

Once we finally knew what was needed, and expected in the app, our team was able to start Lo-fi prototyping. Our team each came up with our own ideas, then we took the best ideas to combine into a few final iterations. This became the basis for our first digital prototype.

Prototyping Iteration 1

The team then each came up with digital prototypes based on the lo-fi prototypes. Joey's was selected to use for usability testing.

Usability Testing

Due to a time crunch, our team was only able to test three participants, but we got similar feedback from each, which we considered invaluable. We tasked users to go through the basic functions of the app, and give feedback not only on how it worked, but on what have made it better.

Prototyping Iteration 2

After testing, we made some revisions based on the comments recieved by participants, the main problems were:

  • It was difficult to tell what was clickable
  • "Willing to Trade" was confusing
  • Lockouts should be present if information is not filled out correctly

These problems were addressed in the second and final iteration.

Final Product

After lots of research, interviews, prototyping and testing, the final version of Swap and Shop was created.

Presentation

The last day of class our team leader (Joey) gave a presentation of the app to the class acting as stakeholders. He went over the process of how we got to where we are, and the reasoning behind it

This was the first time I have ever done a group project of this scope and scale. This was also the first time I have ever been involved in anything to do with user testing and interviews. I have to say the whole process was very intimidating at first, but my group helped me get through it. I ended up enjoying the process over time, and I'm glad I have some experience under my belt moving forward.
